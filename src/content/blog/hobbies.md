my hobbies compounded

It started with lifting. 

My college housemates and I would go down to the basement, doing dumbbell rows using one of their ex’ dumbbell and bench set he left behind. 

Then came running. My then housemate sent me a 10K as in a haha your company sponsored this, but I took it as a haha let’s sign up. Lifting doesn’t help my cardiovascular health. I ran it, felt like I was going to die in the middle, but felt on top of the world at the end, so I signed up for a half. As I ramp up my mileage to train for a half, early morning runs became the norm in the dreadful summer heat. At the same time, my then boyfriend gifted me a 

matcha set. My first 5 cups were truly horrendous. Clumpy and too much milk. Since I drink this every day, I experimented and improved over time. I started to sift my matcha, perfected the water temperature, whisk in a M shape for the microfoam, and perfected my milk to matcha ratio. I developed a caffeine dependency. 

The best matcha is from the tried and true ratio of 1:10 water to matcha. Swirl it, then whisk in an M shape. 

However, I can’t drink matcha everyday if I want to watch my iron levels. But I must keep my caffeine addiction. At first I used a phin drip. Then I feel like I am giving disrespect to the flavors of the coffee beans by merely making a concentrate through phin. Pour overs are the best way extract and actually taste the flavor. So I bought a hario switch. Wait! Coffee tastes best when grinder fresh. I am going to Taiwan soon, let me buy a 1Zpresso hand grinder. Now I am learning about the different types of beans and different grind sizes and brew times.

I alternate between a matcha and a pour over every day. That’s my caffeine addiction. 
Then I got laid off.

Suddenly, my hobbies that compounded, collapsed. Why would I grind coffee and drink matcha if I don’t have to force myself to wake up after 6 hours of sleep anymore? Even running took a tiny hit. Why do I run if I don’t have any races coming up? Then I realize I need it for sanity purposes.

As some time passed and I no longer have to wake up these early mornings, I still found myself craving the smell of matcha or coffee as the hot water hit the powder, filling the kitchen with a wonderful aroma that functioned as my real alarm clock. Even though I don’t really _need_ caffeine anymore, I still yearn for it. That’s when I realized 

The _why_ is so important. The only hobbies that I have a genuine passion for, stuck, while those that are more habits, fell as my old habits collapsed. A friend once said you can have a lot of hobbies and still be a boring person. That happens when there’s no intention nor reason behind the action. For example, if someone treats pilates as just because everyone else is doing it, they can’t really talk at length, passionately, about it. Bring back rambling, the type where you can talk on and on, even if the sentence structure and order of operations doesn’t make sense because of the sheer passion of the hobby. In the case of making drinks, I love the experimentation of it. Adjusting the temperature, the milk, the powder, can make such a difference, and tasting that difference helped me get out of bed every morning.

As I will embark on my journey of traveling, some of my hobbies won’t make sense to do at the moment. For instance, I will not be bringing my matcha powder and set up with already heavy bag. That doesn’t matter though, as 

hobbies are only the _what_ of the why. For instance, I like challenging myself and seeing pretty sights. I can do that with biking, running, or hiking (I in fact, do all three). I like listening to new stories and challenging my biases, but I can do that traveling or even just reading. The _what_ changes but the _why_ stays relatively the same. 

That’s why my front page has a picture of a cafe containing my hobbies. Hobbies come and go like the customers of a cafe, but these are the ones I currently have. 

As I bring in a new routine of uncertainty, what will my hobbies be and how will they compound?
